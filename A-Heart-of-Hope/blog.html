Why a Heart of Hope



Countless people have lost their lives to domestic violence crimes in the United States – not only intimate partners, but
their children, parents, grandparents, grandchildren, siblings, aunts, uncles, friends, neighbors, new partners, and co-
workers. Countless more have been injured – many critically. Most recover – some from devastating injuries. Some
must deal with life-long injuries, such as disfigurement, paralyzation, or brain injuries. Some live, but do not, and will
not, recover from their injuries. Many more lives are ravaged by other forms of abuse. This epidemic is far worse than
most people realize.
There is much work to be done when it comes to domestic violence. Victims need to know how to recognize abuse and
how to get out safety, potential victims need to be knowledgeable about abuse in order to prevent themselves from
becoming victimized, and many survivors need knowledge and support to start over. But awareness efforts cannot
stop there. We must also raise awareness about our system’s successes and failures and urge the public to demand
higher standards of our more than 20,000 separate justice systems in the U.S., which includes thousands upon
thousands of lawmakers, law enforcement officers, prosecutors, judges, magistrates, court clerks, corrections
personnel, emergency/911/dispatch, medical professionals, advocates, and others across the United States. The sad
truth is that there are very few experts on domestic violence in our systems. Systems in some communities do not
have even one expert on domestic violence. As a result, we are seeing the same mistakes being made over and over
and over again across the United States.
There are a number of programs that our communities could be, and should be, using to stem the tide of domestic
violence. There are Coordinated Community Response programs, Lethality assessment programs, domestic violence
training programs, Bystander Intervention programs, Domestic violence and dating violence educational programs,
Batterer’s Intervention programs, and Family Justice Centers, to name just a few. We need EVERY community in the
United States to become proactive in their response to domestic violence. This is no easy task given that our system is
so fractured. There are more than 3,000 counties; more than 20,000 law enforcement agencies and as many courts
all operating independently of one another – with their own policies and their own staff who may or may not be
knowledgeable about domestic violence.
A Heart of Hope Foundation is building an awareness campaign that will make it impossible for people to continue
to ignore, minimize or deny just how deadly domestic violence can be. We are working on educating not only the system,
but the public, about what their communities could be, and should be, doing to curb domestic violence. It’s time we
demand much higher standards from our taxpayer-funded public services.
If we don’t learn from our mistakes, we are doomed to keep repeating them, and people will continue to die in large
numbers.
